Movies

pudge on 2001-12-04T13:38:28

"Philosopher" is a word that a lot of people don't use, and when they do, they envision either an old guy with suede patches on his elbows sitting in a musty university office, surrounded by books, smoking a pipe, and talking about Aristotle, or they envision a young man not yet graduated from university, wearing beat-up t-shirts and long hair in a ponytail, sitting with his legs crossed on the lawn, talking about "the system."

As to Madness of George III, well, many people go to movies without really knowing what they are about. So yeah, the title makes a difference. Coppola wanted to call The Godfather Part III something else, because he did not see it as a traditional sequel; but the studio would have none of it. They figured, and probably rightly so, that more people would see it if it were called The Godfather Part III. It's not so much about people being stupid, but about people scanning the newspaper for a movie to see, and skipping over unfamiliar titles that don't immediately jump out at them. "The Godfather Part III" will jump out at you. "Madness of George III", especially in a country where we don't really know much of British royalty outside of Di and DoDi, is not immediately revealing to many people.

from ebert's movie answer man:


Q. It's said no one ever went broke underestimating the intelligence of the American public. ''Harry Potter and the Philosopher's Stone'' is an excellent film up here in Canada, as was ''The Madness of George III.'' I wonder if the same can be said for the U.S. releases ''Harry Potter and the Sorcerer's Stone'' and ''The Madness of King George.'' The point of course, is that these minor title details were changed for American audiences on the assumption they are too stupid to handle the concept of the philosopher's stone of alchemical fame, or to realize seeing George III doesn't mean you've missed parts I and II. Are these decisions made because the suits think I'm really dumb, or is it because they are?


A. Let's say they think you're as dumb as they are when they make the decisions. The Harry Potter title change was made not by Warner Brothers but by the book publishers, Scholastic, who feared American readers might be scared off by the word ''philosopher.''
